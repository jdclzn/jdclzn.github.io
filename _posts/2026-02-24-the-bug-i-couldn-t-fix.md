**Drafts & Dragons** — *Field Notes from a Developer Who Learned Not Everything Can Be Debugged*

![Drafts & Dragons](/assets/postbg/clouds.png)

I’ve spent most of my life believing that everything is fixable.

Not easily. Not quickly. But fixable.

That’s the quiet religion of software developers.

Systems fail. You investigate.

Features break. You patch.

Servers crash. You restart.

Even when things are on fire, there’s comfort in knowing the fire has a source.

There’s always a root cause.

There’s always a commit that introduced the bug.

There’s always a version where things still worked.

You just have to find it.

You just have to go back far enough.

---

## Mid-January: The First Warning Log

It was the middle of January 2026.

A Wednesday, I think.

Or maybe a Thursday.

It’s strange. I can remember error messages from five years ago, but I can’t remember the exact day my life started splitting into before and after.

My mother got sick.

Not violently. Not dramatically.

Just… wrong.

Like a system running slower than usual.

Still functional.

Still responding.

But you know something underneath is struggling.

We told ourselves it was nothing.

Just stress.

Just fatigue.

Just something that would resolve on its own.

We’ve always been good at lying to ourselves when the truth feels too expensive.

---

## February: The Hidden Variable

Then February came.

And with it, the kind of truth that doesn’t arrive all at once.

It arrives in fragments.

Incomplete data.

Partial logs.

Disconnected symptoms.

Until suddenly, the pattern becomes undeniable.

She had a lump in her breast.

And not just recently.

Not weeks.

Not months.

Three years.

Three years she carried it.

Three years she said nothing.

Three years the process ran silently in the background, consuming resources, spreading threads into places it didn’t belong.

Stage IV breast cancer.

By the time we discovered it, the system wasn’t just compromised.

It was everywhere.

There was no clean rollback.

No restore point.

No last known good state.

---

## The Commit She Never Told Us About

I asked her why.

Not as a developer.

As a son.

Why didn’t you tell us?

She didn’t answer immediately.

She just looked at me with the kind of sadness that doesn’t need words.

And then I remembered.

Three years ago, my little brother died.

Brain aneurysm.

One moment he was there.

The next moment, he wasn’t.

No warning.

No deprecation notice.

No graceful shutdown.

Just sudden termination.

Fatal error.

Process killed.

Core dumped.

Our family never recovered.

We just kept running.

Broken, but running.

And somewhere inside that grief, my mother made a decision.

She would hide the lump.

She would hide the pain.

She would hide the possibility that she might leave us too.

Because she couldn’t bear to deploy another catastrophe into our lives.

So she contained it.

Or tried to.

She carried the exception silently.

Unhandled.

---

## Meanwhile, I Was Refactoring My Life

While cancer was spreading inside her, I was doing code reviews.

I was arguing about naming conventions.

I was optimizing database queries to save milliseconds.

Milliseconds.

I was proud of saving milliseconds.

While she was losing years.

I was building scalable systems.

Designing fault-tolerant architectures.

Planning for failure scenarios.

But I never planned for this.

No one teaches you how to design for the failure of the people you love.

---

## The Guilt Is a Memory Leak

I keep thinking about all the signs I missed.

All the warnings I ignored.

All the times she said she was tired.

All the times she said she wasn’t feeling well.

All the times I said, “I’m busy.”

Busy.

What a meaningless word.

I was busy shipping features that no longer matter.

Busy fixing bugs no one will remember.

Busy being a developer.

While she was quietly dying.

The guilt doesn’t crash you all at once.

It leaks.

Slowly.

Consuming everything.

Until one day you realize you don’t recognize yourself anymore.

---

## There Is No Production Fix

In software, even the worst disasters have procedures.

You roll back.

You restore from backup.

You patch forward.

You mitigate.

You recover.

But cancer doesn’t work like that.

There is no hotfix.

There is no emergency patch.

There is no engineer on call who can log in and save her.

There is no version of reality where I can revert to before she got sick.

Before my brother died.

Before everything started breaking.

I keep wishing I could just go back to that commit.

Just one checkout.

Just one more chance.

---

## I Am Still Writing Code

And that’s the strangest part.

I am still writing code.

I still open my laptop.

Still say things like, “I’ll push a fix later.”

Still pretend the world is normal.

Because the world doesn’t stop.

Production doesn’t stop.

Time doesn’t stop.

Even when your heart does.

Sometimes I’m in the middle of debugging something, and it hits me.

This doesn’t matter !!.

None of this matters !!.

Not the feature !!.

Not the deadline !!.

Not the sprint !!.

Because somewhere in the same moment, my mother is fighting a battle she fought alone for three years.

Three years.

Alone.

---

## Drafts & Dragons

I named this blog Drafts & Dragons because software development always felt like a fantasy.

You start with nothing.

You build worlds.

You create systems.

You fight monsters.

You level up.

You believe, eventually, you become powerful enough to protect what matters.

But this is the dragon I can’t fight.

This is the enemy that doesn’t care about my level.

Doesn’t care about my experience.

Doesn’t care how many systems I’ve built.

This is the part of the story where the hero realizes he was never in control.

---

## Time Is Not Infinite

We live like time is infinite.

Like there’s always another release.

Another sprint.

Another year.

Another chance.

But there isn’t.

There are no guarantees.

No SLAs.

No uptime commitments.

Just borrowed time.

And someday, without warning, the system shuts down.

---

## I Don’t Know How This Story Ends

I wish I could write a better ending.

I wish I could say we caught it in time.

I wish I could say there’s a fix.

I wish I could say everything will be okay.

But this isn’t code.

This is real life.

And real life doesn’t care about clean endings.

Right now, all I can do is stay.

Sit beside her.

Talk to her.

Listen.

Be present.

Not as a developer.

Not as an engineer.

Not as someone who fixes things.

Just as her son.

Because in the end, that’s the only role that ever mattered.

---

## If You’re Reading This

Call your parents.

Visit them.

Sit with them.

Don’t wait.

Don’t assume there’s time.

Because one day, without warning, you’ll discover a bug you can’t fix.

And you’ll spend the rest of your life wishing you had found it sooner.
