**Drafts & Dragons** — *Field Notes from a Developer Learning to Manage Systems Beyond Control*
![Field Notes](/assets/postbg/mountains.png)

The last three entries were about **fatal errors**:

* **The Bug I Couldn’t Fix** — discovery of the unhandled exception in life.
* **The Last Backup** — archiving what could not be restored.
* **Graceful Shutdown** — witnessing a process terminate with dignity.

And now, the log asks me a new question:

**What next?**

---

## Accepting the Production Environment

I’ve spent my career in controlled environments.

- Staging servers
- Automated build and deployment pipelines
- Monitored deployments

But this… this is production.

No staging environment.
No rollback.
No simulated test cases.

The system is running.
My mother is the main process.
It’s handling tasks we can’t monitor.
And I am an on-call developer who can’t patch the kernel.

Acceptance isn’t defeat.
It’s acknowledging **runtime constraints** I can’t override.

I can’t debug life.
I can only interface with it.

---

## Priorities as Critical Resources

In software, the highest-priority threads get CPU time.
Everything else waits.

Life works the same way.

Before, I optimized trivial things:

* SQL queries for milliseconds
* Features for velocity
* Refactors for elegance

Now, my CPU cycles are allocated differently:

* Listening to her stories
* Holding her hand
* Being present for small transactions of affection
* Logging memory of moments

Everything else is a low-priority process.
I suspend them.
They will wait in the queue.

---

## Memory Management

Memory leaks are fatal in code.

I realized that human memory leaks too — especially under stress, grief, and distraction.

So I log everything:

* Her voice at 7:03 AM
* How she laughs when she tells stories of my little brother
* The pattern of her gestures when worried

I am creating a **commit history of existence**.

Not for version control.
Not for rollback.
But for the only thing worse than loss: forgetting.

---

## Handling Exceptions Gracefully

I cannot prevent runtime errors in this system.

But I can handle them.

* If a moment turns into anxiety → catch it, don’t propagate
* If a memory triggers panic → encapsulate and log
* If grief interrupts my focus → delegate lower-priority threads

I have no root cause fix.
No patch.
Only exception handling and recovery strategies.

---

## Running in Maintenance Mode

I can’t schedule downtime for life.

But I can enter **maintenance mode**:

* Pause other projects
* Suspend deployments
* Focus on the highest-priority process: **her**

This is real production support.
Real-life uptime management.
No alerts will appear in Slack.
No CI pipelines will help me here.

---

## Time as a Limited Resource

In software, we profile bottlenecks.
We optimize critical paths.
We prevent system overload.

Time is my critical path now.

* Every hour counts
* Every conversation counts
* Every presence counts

Because unlike code, **time cannot be recompiled**.

Every missed moment is a non-recoverable exception.

---

## Forward, Not Back

“What next?” isn’t a function I can call with predictable output.

* I cannot guarantee recovery
* I cannot guarantee uptime
* I cannot guarantee another day

I can only commit to:

* Love fully, without rollback
* Listen actively, without buffering
* Log moments, without truncation

Even when I cannot control the system, I can respect it.
Even when I cannot patch the pain, I can witness it.
Even when I cannot prevent loss, I can ensure it is surrounded by care.

---

## The Next Chapter

The code of life does not compile without errors.
The system runs in production, with all its unpredictability.

All I can do is:

* Stay connected
* Prioritize the critical process
* Allocate resources to presence
* Keep running my own thread alongside hers

Until the shutdown signal comes…

I will keep the logs, run the processes I can, and maintain uptime in the only environment that matters: **our shared moments**.

And when the final exception occurs… I will make sure the last operation is filled with love.

Because some bugs you can’t fix.

Some crashes you can’t prevent.

But some uptime… some presence… you can give entirely, without fail.
